Collatz Series, proof of termination.

An alternate description of the Collatz sequence is: "the next element is one plus 3 times the previous element, then remove the trailing zeroes". Additionally we require the initial value to be odd.
C0 is odd
Cn+1 = 1+Cn+2*Cn , then divide by 2 until the result is odd (discard all factors of 2).
Note for hardware implementation: Multiplying by 3 is achievable by shifting the previous element up one and adding to the unshifted prior value, and the "one plus" part achievable by filling the lsb of the shifted number with 1.

The conjecture to prove is that the series converges to 1 in a finite number of steps when starting at a finite value.

The number of bits needed to represent a value in base 2 is ~1+log2(value). If the conjecture is true then in a finite number of steps the number of bits needed becomes 1.
  1. The '1+3*' part of each cycle adds at most 2 bits to the number, a multiply by 3 and adding one can at most (and only for one case) increase the number by a factor of 4.
  2. Since the number is always odd, multiplying by 3 yields an odd value, adding the one makes the number even and at least one bit is removed each cycle.
  3. Hence the number of bits need for Cn+1 is at most 1+bits needed for Cn

There are two special pairs: the most significant one, whose number of zeros is 0 or otherwise irrelevant; and the least significant one, which suffers a subset of the cases that intermediate pairs do. For conciseness I will call the least significant pair the trailing pair, the most significant pair the leading pair.

Four values of Ones[] are interesting:
  1. N(1), the number of isolated 1 bits.
  2. N(2), the number of isolated pairs of 1 bits.
  3. The sum of all remaining N's: Nother.
  4. The sum of all of the above: Ntotal.

We will explore how the above numbers change at each step of the sequence.

The finite number of cases is the product of: [1,2,>2] zeroes followed by [1,2,>2] ones followed by [1,2,>2] zeroes. The leading and trailing groups are a subset of these.
This breakdown is adequate as multiplying by 3 adds at most 2 bits to a number, a leading 10 can form, adding a 1 (either because it is the trailing group or because the group below it was only spaced off by 1 zero) will not cause a 3rd bit to be needed, at most the leading bits become 11.
